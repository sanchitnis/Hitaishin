# 360-Degree Feedback Protocol for JP Alumni
## Seeing Yourself Through Others' Eyes — a Prabodhini Svadhyaya Tool

---

> *"The Prajna that comes from one's own examination is incomplete*
> *without the mirror that others hold."*
> — In the spirit of JP's multi-perspective Paristhiti Jnana tradition

---

## Purpose

Self-assessment is the foundation of coaching. But the JP leader has a characteristic blind spot:
high intelligence and self-awareness create the illusion of self-knowledge while certain
patterns remain invisible from the inside.

The 360-feedback process surfaces:
- **Impact gaps** — the difference between *intended* impact and *actual* impact on others
- **Invisible strengths** — capacities others see clearly that the leader undervalues
- **Organizational Attitude blind spots** — which OA component looks different to colleagues than to the leader
- **Adaptive challenges** — recurring themes in feedback that signal a deeply rooted pattern, not a skill gap

**JP alignment**: This is applied Paristhiti Jnana — reading one's own situation accurately using
multiple vantage points. It is rigorous Svadhyaya, not merely introspection.

---

## When to Use

| Trigger | When to Run a 360 |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Phase 3 or 4** | After 3+ months of coaching, once a stable profile exists to receive feedback against |
| **New role or team** | When a leader has taken on a new institutional role and wants to understand early perceptions |
| **Recurring leadership challenge** | When the same team/organizational challenge keeps recurring despite attempted fixes |
| **Annual retreat** | As input to the yearly Svadharma and OA recalibration (Session 3.5) |
| **Mentee request** | Any time the mentee senses a gap between their self-perception and others' experience |

**Do not use** during crisis, conflict, or when the leader is emotionally destabilized — the feedback requires psychological stability to receive well.

## The JP-Adapted 360 Structure

This protocol is lightweight by design — 5 questions, 3–5 respondents, 20 minutes per respondent.
It is not a corporate HR process. It is a Svadhyaya tool.

### Step 1: Choose Respondents (3–5 people)

Select respondents who:
- Know the leader in a real working or relational context
- Will tell the truth (not manage up or protect feelings)
- Represent different spheres of the leader's life (not all professional contacts)

**Recommended mix** (pick 3–5):
| Type | Why |
|------|-----|
| One direct report or team member | How you lead, not just what you decide |
| One peer at similar level | How you collaborate, compete, and cooperate |
| One senior (mentor, manager, board member) | How you show up under institutional hierarchy |
| One family member (spouse, adult child) — *optional* | How the home sphere experiences the leader |
| One SPG/community collaborator — *if applicable* | How the leader shows up in volunteer/social contribution space |

**Exclude**: people who are in active conflict with the leader; people who lack enough context; people who would cause harm to include (e.g., toxic relationship).

### Step 2: The 5-Question Instrument

Send this directly (email, WhatsApp, or in person) to each respondent. Keep it anonymous if the mentee prefers — but named feedback from trusted peers is often more actionable.

---

**Hitaishin 360 — [Leader Name] — [Date]**

*Thank you for taking 20 minutes for this. Your honest perspective is a genuine gift.
There are no right or wrong answers. The only wrong answer is a polite non-answer.*

**Q1. What is [Name]'s greatest strength — the quality that shows up most consistently and creates the most value for others?**
*(Be specific. Not "he is smart" but "when she is in a difficult meeting, she has the rare ability to hear what is not being said and name it.")*

**Q2. Where does [Name]'s impact fall short of their intention?**
*(What do they think they're doing that isn't landing the way they mean it to?
This is not criticism — it is data.)*

**Q3. In which of these Organizational Attitude components do you see [Name] as strongest — and which as an area for growth?**
- Lakshya Nishtha (staying committed to goals under pressure)
- Anushasan (consistent personal discipline and high standards)
- Sahakar (genuinely collaborative, enables others to lead)
- Seva Bhava (fundamentally oriented toward serving others, not recognition)
- Sanghatan (actively builds teams and institutions, not just outputs)

*(You can simply name the strongest and weakest.)*

**Q4. What is one thing [Name] could do differently that would significantly increase their impact on the team/community/work?**
*(One concrete, specific behavioral suggestion.)*

**Q5. What do you most appreciate about working with or being in relationship with [Name]?**
*(This question is as important as the growth-edge questions. Honest appreciation is data too.)*

---

### Step 3: Synthesize — The Coaching Preparation

Before sharing the feedback with the mentee, the coach (or the mentee solo, if self-administering):

1. **Cluster the responses**: What themes appear across 2+ respondents? Single-respondent observations may be idiosyncratic; recurring themes are signal.

2. **Map to OA Framework**: Which OA component(s) appear in both the strength data and the gap data? This is the development frontier.

3. **Identify the impact gap**: Compare the mentee's self-assessment of their impact to what respondents describe. Where is the gap widest?

4. **Look for the invisible strength**: What do respondents name as a strength that the mentee has never mentioned or actively undervalues? This is often the most powerful discovery.

5. **Identify the adaptive challenge**: If the same theme appears in multiple responses despite the leader's prior attempts to address it, it is likely an adaptive challenge (values/identity-rooted), not a technical one.

Prepare a **3-point synthesis**:
- The central strength (what's working at its best)
- The central impact gap (the difference between intention and effect)
- The adaptive hypothesis (the deeper belief or pattern driving the gap)

---

### Step 4: The Feedback Conversation (45–60 min)

This is Session 4.2 territory if it triggers a Svadharma pivot, or a standalone coaching session.

**Structure**:
1. **Ground the mentee** (5 min) — Pranayama or a brief settling. This is Svadhyaya, not a performance review.
2. **Share the synthesis first** (10 min) — Not raw quotes. The synthesized three-point picture.
3. **Explore the strength** (10 min) — "What is your reaction to what others see as your core strength? Does it surprise you?"
4. **Explore the impact gap** (15 min) — "What is your reaction to the gap? What do you think is driving it?" (Resist the temptation to explain or defend — ask the leader to sit with it first.)
5. **The adaptive challenge hypothesis** (10 min) — "If this gap persists despite your good intentions, what belief or identity investment might be maintaining it?"
6. **One commitment** (10 min) — What is one specific behavior change the mentee is willing to try for 30 days?

**Coaching stance**: Hold the space. Don't rush past discomfort. The moment of sitting with
uncomfortable truth — without explaining it away — is where the most powerful learning happens.

---

### Step 5: Follow-Up

- Add the 360 synthesis to the mentee's profile under **"360 Feedback — [Date]"**
- Review at the next Quarterly Review: has the adaptive challenge shifted?
- Run a second 360 after 12 months if the mentee wishes to track change

## Integration with the OA Leadership Profile

The psychometric OA test (from `intake/apps/03-leadership-oa-profile.html`) gives the mentee's
*self-assessed* OA profile. The 360's Q3 gives *peer-assessed* OA perception.

**The gap between these two profiles is highly diagnostic:**

| Pattern | What it suggests |
|---------|-----------------|
| Self-score high on Sahakar, peers score low | Collaboration is valued but not experienced — leader controls without noticing |
| Self-score low on Seva Bhava, peers score high | Imposter syndrome: the leader serves more than they give themselves credit for |
| Self and peers agree on weakness | Clear development edge, high self-awareness |
| Self and peers disagree across the board | Low external feedback sensitivity — the feedback conversation itself is the coaching |

## A Note on Philosophical Grounding

In the JP tradition, receiving feedback is not a vulnerability — it is an act of Sraddha.
The leader who seeks truth about themselves from others, and can hold it without defensiveness
or collapse, embodies what Vivekananda called *Abhayam* (fearlessness) in its most mature form.

The instruction to seek feedback is itself ancient:
> *"Acharya devo bhava"* — The teacher is divine.
> Every person who offers you honest feedback is, in that moment, your teacher.

The leader's task is to *receive the teaching*, regardless of the form it comes in.
